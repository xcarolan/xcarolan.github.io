As an engineer, I'm trained to spot patterns. When a system throws an error immediately after a specific input, you don't assume coincidence. You trace the connection. You look for causality, even when it's uncomfortable.

The last 72 hours have presented us with one of those patterns.

## The Timeline That Caught My Attention

On February 24th, the Department of Justice released the long-awaited Epstein files. The documents contained millions of pages of investigative material. But within days, journalists discovered something significant was missing: [three FBI memos describing interviews with a woman who accused Donald Trump of sexual abuse when she was a minor](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/feb/26/trump-epstein-files-fbi).

The Guardian reviewed the missing documents — 25 pages of agents' notes from four interviews conducted in 2019. The story broke. Members of Congress demanded answers. An investigation was launched by congressional Democrats. The administration dismissed the allegations as "non-credible" and "duplicative," but the documents remained absent from the public release.

Then, less than 48 hours after this revelation, [President Trump ordered military strikes against Iran](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/feb/28/iran-attack-us-political-reaction).

## The Pattern We Keep Seeing

I'm not claiming to know what was in the President's mind when he ordered those strikes. That's not something I can prove, and speculation without evidence is how democracies lose their grip on reality.

But I can tell you what the pattern looks like from a systems perspective.

February 24-26: Damaging news breaks about missing documents related to serious accusations.

February 28: Major military action launches with minimal congressional notification and virtually no public case-building.

The strikes were announced via Truth Social video. Congress received what Senator Tim Kaine described as "zero" meaningful notice — just a call to the Speaker of the House. A Senate vote on a war powers resolution, scheduled for the following week, was effectively preempted.

## Why the Pattern Matters

In 1997, the film "Wag the Dog" depicted a fictional president manufacturing a war to distract from a scandal. The phrase entered our lexicon because the pattern is recognizable: when leadership faces existential political threat, external conflict becomes suddenly urgent.

The problem isn't that this happens. The problem is that we've normalized it.

We've created a system where military action can be launched with less procedural rigor than a municipal zoning variance. Where congressional oversight is reduced to a phone call. Where the public receives three minutes of explanation in a 108-minute speech.

As an engineer, I look for feedback loops. This is a dangerous one: the incentive to escalate militarily increases precisely when accountability mechanisms are closing in. That's not a bug in the system. That's a feature of broken governance.

## What the Research Shows

Historical analysis of "diversionary war" theory — the academic term for using military conflict to shift domestic political attention — shows mixed results. Some studies find correlation between scandal and military action. Others don't. The data is messy because motivations are opaque.

What we can verify: the timing is suspiciously convenient. What we cannot: the intent behind it.

But here's what I keep coming back to: even if the timing is purely coincidental, the *capacity* for this pattern to exist is the problem. When a president can order strikes without meaningful congressional consultation, when the public receives minimal explanation, when checks and balances are reduced to theater — we've already lost something essential regardless of motivation.

## The Fix Isn't Proving Intent

We're not going to get a memo saying "launch strikes to distract from Epstein story." If such calculations happen, they happen in rooms without witnesses, transmitted through implication and incentive rather than explicit instruction.

So what do we do?

**1. Demand procedural guardrails, not just outcomes.** I don't care which party holds the White House. Military action should require actual congressional authorization under the War Powers Resolution. Not a phone call. Not a notification. A vote. Senator Kaine and Senator Rand Paul have bipartisan legislation to enforce this. Support it regardless of your politics.

**2. Track the timing yourself.** Don't let the news cycle wash over you. When major scandals break, watch what happens 48-72 hours later. Not to indulge conspiracy theories, but to maintain civic awareness. Patterns emerge when you zoom out.

**3. Support journalism that follows both stories.** The Epstein files investigation continues. The Iran strikes require ongoing scrutiny. These shouldn't be treated as separate news cycles. They're interconnected data points in a pattern of executive power.

**4. Contact your representatives about war powers.** This sounds small. It isn't. Congress has ceded authority to the executive branch for decades. Reclaiming it requires constituent pressure. One email per scandal isn't enough. Sustained, boring, persistent advocacy is what changes systems.

## The Cathedral and the Congregation

I wrote last week about building infrastructure for projects that don't have users yet. This is the inverse problem: we have the infrastructure of war — the vast armada, the weapons systems, the command chains — but we've neglected the infrastructure of democratic decision-making.

The congregation here is the American public. We're being asked to accept military action without explanation, to trust that timing is coincidental, to believe that accountability is happening somewhere behind closed doors.

I don't accept that. Neither should you.

The pattern isn't proof of motive. But the pattern *is* proof of vulnerability. Our system allows this. Our system enables distraction, enables rushed military action, enables the subordination of legislative oversight to executive impulse.

Fixing that requires more than outrage at any one president. It requires rebuilding the guardrails we've allowed to erode.
